About this analysis

What was studied?

This project analyses a real Google Ads dataset for a "Data Analytics Course" running through all of 2024. The data covers 6 keywords, 3 device types (Desktop, Mobile, Tablet), and every day of the week — cleaned column by column, feature-engineered, and visualised using Python.

ROASReturn on Ad Spend — for every $1 spent on ads, how many dollars came back as revenue. 7× means $7 earned per $1 spent.
ROIReturn on Investment — profit as a percentage of cost. 590% means the campaign made ~6× its cost in pure profit.
CTRClick-Through Rate — out of 100 people who saw the ad, how many clicked it.
CPCCost Per Click — how much was paid each time someone clicked the ad.
CPLCost Per Lead — how much it costs to get one person to enquire or sign up.

Anomaly investigation

The November Spike

November stood out immediately from the first monthly table. First step was verifying whether this was real data or a data quality issue.

Anomaly Detected Was it bad data — or a real campaign push?
November showed $2.98M revenue and $426K spend — roughly 27× higher than any other month. First check: cost-per-record in November averaged $214.63 vs $216.58 for other months. Consistent. Device and keyword splits inside November were proportional to the full year. Conclusion — this was a genuine planned budget scale-up, not corrupted data.

Metric relationships

How Metrics Connect

A correlation heatmap showed how key metrics move together — and some findings were counterintuitive.

Correlation Between Key Metrics
Closer to 1.0 = strong positive · Closer to −1.0 = move in opposite directions
📈
Cost ↔ Revenue: highly correlated
Spend drives revenue fairly linearly. The campaign scales predictably — more budget = more revenue at roughly the same ROAS.
↕️
Clicks ↔ Conversion Rate: weakly correlated
More clicks do NOT automatically mean better conversion rate. High-traffic campaigns often attract lower-intent audiences. Landing page quality matters more than traffic volume.
💡
Conversions ↔ Conversion Rate: strong positive
When conversion rate is high, total conversions are also high. Improving the rate — through better ad copy or landing pages — has a compounding effect.
